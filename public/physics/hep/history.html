A Brief History of Particle Physics

The earliest part of this history is probably familiar to most from high school chemistry class, nevertheless, it is included here for completeness.

1895 - Roentgen discovers x-rays
1897 - J. J. Thompson discovers electrons
1909 - Rutherford scatters alpha particles off gold foil to demonstrate atoms have nuclei
1920 - Rutherford postulates the existance of the neutron to explain isotopes
1927 - Quantum Mechanics reaches maturity
1930 - Pauli postulates the existance of the neutrino to preserve the conservation of energy in beta decay (Bohr favors dumping the conservation of energy; fortunately Pauli turns out to be right)
1932 - Chadwick proves the existance of the neutron (Nature 129, 312 (1932))
1932 - Carl Anderson discovers the first antiparticle, the positron, vindicating Dirac's theory of the electron (Phys. Rev 43, 491 (1933))

This is the part where most people's historical knowledge ends. Not coincidentally, it is at this point that story also starts to get very messy and stays so until the Standard Model is completely formed almost 50 years later.

In the early days particle physicists had only one source to probe matter with, naturally occuring radiation discovered by the Curies in the form of alpha, beta and gamma particles (later realized to be helium nuclei, electrons and photons respectively). All the early experiments used radioactive sources until the discovery of cosmic rays by ? in 1912. The theory at the time was that all the radiation at the earth's surface was caused by the radioactive decay of elements within the earth. To test this, ? went up in a hot air balloon with a Geiger counter(?) and found that the level of radiation increased with altitude. The atmosphere filters out most cosmic rays, so there are more at higher altitudes. Carl Anderson used a cloud chamber pointed upward to study these cosmic rays and it was in these rays that he discovered the first positron.

1934 - Cherenkov effect discovered
1935 - Yukawa postulates theory of nuclear forces
1936 - Breit-Wigner resonance formula developed

1937 - Discovery of mesons in cosmic rays:
S. H. Neddermeyer and C. D. Anderson, Phys. Rev. 51, 884 (1937).
J. C. Street and E. C. Stevenson, Phys. Rev. 52, 1003 (1937).

1940 - Spin-Statistics theorem

1943 - Discovery of first strange particles in cosmic rays:
L. LePrince et M. L'héritier, Comptes Rendus Acad. Sci. de Paris, 618 (1944).

1945 - Two atomic bombs detonated against Japan
1946 - The meson hypothesized to be two particles (pions and muons)
1947 - pions conclusively obeserved in cosmic rays

Ih the early 1950's particle accelerators began to supercede cosmic rays as a major source of particles for study. The first cyclotron had been constructed in 1931.

Discovery of the first strange baryon was made by the Cosmotron at Brookhaven National Lab (W. B. Fowler et al, Phys. Rev. 93, 861 (1954).)

CP violation was first observed by Christenson et al in the neutral Kaon system (Phys. Rev. Lett. 13, 138 (1964)).

1974 - J/Psi resonance discovered
1977 - Upsilon resonance discovered (S. W. Herb et al, Phys. Rev. Lett. 39, 252 (1977).)
1983 - Weak bosons (Z0, W+, and W-) discovered
1990 - Number of neutrino flavors measured as 3 from Z0 width
1995 - Top quark discovered at Fermilab

References:
